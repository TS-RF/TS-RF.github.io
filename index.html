Thermodynamic simulation-assisted random forest: Towards explainable fault diagnosis of combustion chamber components of marine diesel engines

Abstract

Aiming at the challenges that traditional intelligent fault diagnosis methods of marine diesel engines often suffer low generalizability due to the lack of fault training samples, as well as poor explainability due to the insufficient incorporation of domain knowledge on fault mechanism, this paper develops a Thermodynamic Simulation-assisted Random Forest (TSRF).

The method reveals fault characteristics through thermodynamic simulations and incorporates them as prior knowledge when designing the intelligent fault diagnosis model. Firstly, five thermodynamic fault models are developed by fine-tuning the essential system parameters. Then, potential thermodynamic indicators of combustion chamber component degradation are identified through numerical simulation results.

By calculating SHapley Additive explanations (SHAP) values, a parameter selection process is conducted to retain only those variables demonstrating significant correlations with fault states. Finally, the selected parameters are leveraged to assess the condition of the combustion chamber. The proposed TSRF achieved exceptional classification performance, illustrating a mean accuracy of 99.07% on the fault dataset.

Motivation

In the field of marine engineering and Prognostics and Health Management (PHM), we constantly grapple with two significant bottlenecks:

  • Data Scarcity: Marine diesel engines are expensive, critical assets. We rarely run them to failure just to collect data. Consequently, there is a severe lack of labeled "fault data" required to train robust machine learning models.
  • The "Black Box" Problem: While Deep Learning is popular, it lacks transparency. If an AI tells a chief engineer, "The cylinder liner is worn," but cannot explain why based on physical parameters, it is difficult to build trust.

Our Solution: To address these challenges, we propose a method called TSRF. We use a physics-based simulation to generate synthetic fault data (solving the scarcity problem) and employ SHAP to visualize the decision-making process.

Methodology

Our workflow consists of four distinct stages as illustrated below:

  1. Thermodynamic Modeling: We built a 1-D thermodynamic model of a 6-cylinder marine diesel engine. We calibrated the model using real-world operational data, ensuring the simulation error remained under 5% for key parameters.
  2. Fault Injection: We simulated five specific combustion chamber faults by fine-tuning physical parameters. This allowed us to generate a comprehensive dataset covering scenarios impossible to collect from a real engine.
  3. Feature Selection via SHAP: Using SHAP values, we quantitatively assessed which of the 14 output parameters actually mattered for diagnosis.
  4. Classification: We trained a Random Forest (RF) classifier using this physics-generated dataset.

Thermodynamic Modeling Details

To ensure high fidelity, we constructed a one-dimensional (1-D) operational cycle simulation. This "Digital Twin" balances physical accuracy with the computational speed required for large-scale dataset generation.

Model Topology

The engine is discretized into a network of flow pipes and functional components:

  • Core Power Unit: 6-cylinder, 2-stroke inline configuration.
  • Air Path System: Intake/Exhaust manifolds connected via complex piping networks.
  • Boosting System: Turbocharger (TC1) coupled with an intercooler (CO1).

Calibration & Validation

Before fault injection, the baseline model was rigorously calibrated against empirical data.

  • Data Source: Real-world vessel data acquired via Data Collecting Module (DCM).
  • Validation: The deviation for critical parameters was maintained within a ±5% error margin.

Fault Injection Mechanism

Fault Type Physical Mechanism Modeling Implementation
F1: Head Cracking Disrupted thermal conduction. Elevating Head Temp ($T_H$) to 346°C.
F2: Piston Ablation Material loss & seal compromise. Increasing Piston Temp ($T_P$) + minor blow-by.
F3: Liner Wear Abrasive wear increases bore. Increasing Bore + substantial blow-by.
F4: Ring Wear Gas leakage only. Modulation of Blow-by Mass Flow Rate.
F5: Ring Sticking Friction & seal failure. Bore change + Elevated Liner Temp + Blow-by.

Explainability Analysis

A key innovation of our work is moving beyond "What is the fault?" to "Why is this the fault?". We demonstrated this capability using a specific case of Piston Ring Wear (F4):

  • Local Interpretation (Waterfall Plot): Our waterfall plot analysis explains specific predictions. The model predicted "Ring Wear" because Blow-by Heat Flow ($P_{06}$) and Mass Flow ($P_{07}$) were abnormal. This aligns perfectly with physics: Ring wear destroys the seal, leading to gas leakage.
  • Global Interpretation (Beeswarm Plot): Our global analysis revealed that low values of Pre-turbo Exhaust Pressure ($P_{11}$) are strong indicators of Ring Wear, consistent with reduced turbine energy.
