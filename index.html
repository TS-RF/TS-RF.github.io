Abstract

Aiming at the challenges that traditional intelligent fault diagnosis methods of marine diesel engines often suffer low generalizability due to the lack of fault training samples, as well as poor explainability due to the insufficient incorporation of domain knowledge on fault mechanism, this paper develops a Thermodynamic Simulation-assisted Random Forest (TSRF).

The method reveals fault characteristics through thermodynamic simulations and incorporates them as prior knowledge when designing the intelligent fault diagnosis model. Firstly, five thermodynamic fault models are developed by fine-tuning the essential system parameters to correspond with the distinct attributes of different faults. Then, potential thermodynamic indicators of combustion chamber component degradation are identified through numerical simulation results.

By calculating SHapley Additive explanations (SHAP) values, a parameter selection process is conducted to retain only those variables demonstrating significant correlations with fault states. Finally, the selected parameters are leveraged to assess the condition of the combustion chamber. The proposed TSRF achieved exceptional classification performance, illustrating a mean accuracy of 99.07% on the fault dataset.

Part 1: The Motivation

In the field of marine engineering and Prognostics and Health Management (PHM), researchers constantly grapple with two significant bottlenecks:

  • Data Scarcity: Marine diesel engines are expensive, critical assets. We rarely run them to failure just to collect data. Consequently, there is a severe lack of labeled "fault data" required to train robust machine learning models.
  • The "Black Box" Problem: While Deep Learning (like CNNs) is popular, it lacks transparency. If an AI tells a chief engineer, "The cylinder liner is worn," but cannot explain why based on physical parameters, the engineer will not trust it. This lack of explainability is a major barrier to industry adoption.

The Solution: The authors propose a method called TSRF (Thermodynamic Simulation-assisted Random Forest). Essentially, they use a physics-based simulation to generate synthetic fault data (solving the scarcity problem) and employ SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to visualize the decision-making process, ensuring it aligns with thermodynamic principles (solving the black box problem).

Part 2: The Methodology (The Workflow)

The workflow consists of four distinct stages as illustrated below:

  1. Digital Twin Construction (Thermodynamic Modeling): Instead of relying solely on a physical test rig, the authors built a 1-D thermodynamic model of a 6-cylinder marine diesel engine. They calibrated the model using real-world operational data, keeping simulation error under 5% for key parameters.
  2. Fault Injection (Simulating the "Sickness"): With a calibrated model, the authors simulated five specific combustion chamber faults (e.g., Head Cracking, Piston Ablation) by fine-tuning physical parameters. This generated a comprehensive dataset covering scenarios impossible to collect from a real engine.
  3. Feature Selection via SHAP: Using SHAP values, the authors quantitatively assessed which of the 14 output parameters actually mattered for diagnosis. They identified that parameters like Exhaust Gas Temperature (P14) and Liner Wall Heat Flow (P05) are the most critical indicators.
  4. Classification (The Diagnosis): They trained a Random Forest (RF) classifier using this physics-generated dataset. The model achieved a mean accuracy of 99.07%, significantly outperforming traditional methods like SVM and KNN.

Part 3: The "Explainability" (Bridging AI and Physics)

This is the most novel part of the paper. It moves beyond "What is the fault?" to "Why is this the fault?". The authors demonstrated this using a specific case of Piston Ring Wear (F4):

  • Local Interpretation (Waterfall Plot): Imagine looking at a single patient's chart. The waterfall plot explains a specific prediction. For example, the model predicted "Ring Wear" because Blow-by Heat Flow (P06) and Blow-by Mass Flow (P07) had specific values that pushed the probability up. This aligns with physics: Ring wear destroys the seal, leading to gas leakage (blow-by).
  • Global Interpretation (Beeswarm Plot): This shows the general rules the model learned across all samples. The analysis revealed that low values of Pre-turbo Exhaust Pressure (P11) are strong indicators of Ring Wear. Physically, this checks out: if rings are worn, gas leaks out of the cylinder, meaning less gas energy goes into the exhaust manifold to spin the turbine.

Part 4: Key Takeaways for Your Research

As a graduate student, here is what you should take away from this paper:

  • Physics-Informed AI is Powerful: You don't always need "Big Data." If you understand the mechanism (thermodynamics), you can use simulations to augment your data. This is a very popular research direction.
  • Explainability (XAI) is Mandatory: It is no longer enough to just have high accuracy. You must prove why your model works. Tools like SHAP are standard for "opening the black box."
  • The "Loop" of Validation: The methodology follows a robust loop: Real Data → Model Calibration → Simulation → AI Training → Physics-based Validation. This is a solid framework for a Master's or PhD thesis.
