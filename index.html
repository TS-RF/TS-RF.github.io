Abstract

Aiming at the challenges that traditional intelligent fault diagnosis methods of marine diesel engines often suffer low generalizability due to the lack of fault training samples, as well as poor explainability due to the insufficient incorporation of domain knowledge on fault mechanism, this paper develops a Thermodynamic Simulation-assisted Random Forest (TSRF).

The method reveals fault characteristics through thermodynamic simulations and incorporates them as prior knowledge. Firstly, five thermodynamic fault models are developed by fine-tuning the essential system parameters. Then, potential thermodynamic indicators are identified through numerical simulation results. By calculating SHapley Additive explanations (SHAP) values, a parameter selection process is conducted. The proposed TSRF achieved exceptional classification performance, illustrating a mean accuracy of 99.07% on the fault dataset.

Methodology & Workflow

The proposed TSRF framework integrates physics-based modeling with data-driven machine learning. Our workflow consists of four distinct stages:

1. Digital Twin Construction

We built a 1-D thermodynamic model of a 6-cylinder marine diesel engine. Unlike black-box models, this allows us to understand the internal state of the engine.

  • Calibration: Calibrated using real-world operational data.
  • Accuracy: Simulation error maintained under 5% for key parameters.

2. Fault Injection

We simulated five specific combustion chamber faults by fine-tuning physical parameters (e.g., modifying heat transfer coefficients for Head Cracking or leakage rates for Ring Wear).

Explainability: Why does it work?

High accuracy is not enough. We use SHAP to open the "black box". The figure below analyzes a specific case of Piston Ring Wear (F4).

  • Local Interpretation (Waterfall Plot): The model predicted "Ring Wear" because Blow-by Heat Flow ($P_{06}$) and Mass Flow ($P_{07}$) were abnormal. This aligns with the physical reality of gas leakage.
  • Global Interpretation (Beeswarm Plot): Lower Pre-turbo Pressure ($P_{11}$) is a strong global indicator for this fault, consistent with thermodynamic principles (leakage $\rightarrow$ less energy for turbine).
