Thermodynamic simulation-assisted random forest: Towards explainable fault diagnosis of combustion chamber components of marine diesel engines

Abstract

Aiming at the challenges that traditional intelligent fault diagnosis methods of marine diesel engines often suffer low generalizability due to the lack of fault training samples, as well as poor explainability due to the insufficient incorporation of domain knowledge on fault mechanism, this paper develops a Thermodynamic Simulation-assisted Random Forest (TSRF).

The method reveals fault characteristics through thermodynamic simulations and incorporates them as prior knowledge when designing the intelligent fault diagnosis model. Firstly, five thermodynamic fault models are developed by fine-tuning the essential system parameters to correspond with the distinct attributes of different faults. Then, potential thermodynamic indicators of combustion chamber component degradation are identified through numerical simulation results.

By calculating SHapley Additive explanations (SHAP) values, a parameter selection process is conducted to retain only those variables demonstrating significant correlations with fault states. Finally, the selected parameters are leveraged to assess the condition of the combustion chamber. The proposed TSRF achieved exceptional classification performance, illustrating a mean accuracy of 99.07% on the fault dataset.

Motivation

In the field of marine engineering and Prognostics and Health Management (PHM), we constantly grapple with two significant bottlenecks:

  • Data Scarcity: Marine diesel engines are expensive, critical assets. We rarely run them to failure just to collect data. Consequently, there is a severe lack of labeled "fault data" required to train robust machine learning models.
  • The "Black Box" Problem: While Deep Learning is popular, it lacks transparency. If an AI tells a chief engineer, "The cylinder liner is worn," but cannot explain why based on physical parameters, it is difficult to build trust. This lack of explainability is a major barrier to industry adoption.

Our Solution: To address these challenges, we propose a method called TSRF. We use a physics-based simulation to generate synthetic fault data (solving the scarcity problem) and employ SHAP to visualize the decision-making process, ensuring it aligns with thermodynamic principles.

Methodology

Our workflow consists of four distinct stages as illustrated below:

  1. Digital Twin Construction: Instead of relying solely on a physical test rig, we built a 1-D thermodynamic model of a 6-cylinder marine diesel engine. We calibrated the model using real-world operational data, ensuring the simulation error remained under 5% for key parameters.
  2. Fault Injection: With our calibrated model, we simulated five specific combustion chamber faults (e.g., Head Cracking, Piston Ablation) by fine-tuning physical parameters. This allowed us to generate a comprehensive dataset covering scenarios impossible to collect from a real engine.
  3. Feature Selection via SHAP: Using SHAP values, we quantitatively assessed which of the 14 output parameters actually mattered for diagnosis.
  4. Classification: We trained a Random Forest (RF) classifier using this physics-generated dataset. Our model achieved a mean accuracy of 99.07%, significantly outperforming traditional methods like SVM and KNN.

Explainability Analysis

A key innovation of our work is moving beyond "What is the fault?" to "Why is this the fault?". We demonstrated this capability using a specific case of Piston Ring Wear (F4):

  • Local Interpretation (Waterfall Plot): Our waterfall plot analysis explains specific predictions. For example, our model predicted "Ring Wear" because Blow-by Heat Flow ($P_{06}$) and Blow-by Mass Flow ($P_{07}$) had specific values that pushed the probability up. This aligns perfectly with physics: Ring wear destroys the seal, leading to gas leakage (blow-by).
  • Global Interpretation (Beeswarm Plot): Our global analysis revealed the general rules the model learned. We found that low values of Pre-turbo Exhaust Pressure ($P_{11}$) are strong indicators of Ring Wear. Physically, this is consistent: if rings are worn, gas leaks out of the cylinder, meaning less gas energy goes into the exhaust manifold to spin the turbine.

Research Highlights

We believe this work offers several key contributions to the field:

Physics-Informed AI

We demonstrate that "Big Data" is not the only path. By understanding the thermodynamic mechanism, we can effectively use simulations to augment data and train robust models.

Explainability (XAI)

High accuracy alone is insufficient. By using tools like SHAP, we "opened the black box," bridging the gap between advanced AI algorithms and practical engineering trust.

Closed-Loop Validation

Real Data → Calibration → Simulation → AI Training → Validation. This framework serves as a solid foundation for future PHM research.
